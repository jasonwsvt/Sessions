Energy Testing - Statement Semantics

Number comparison is another area of statement semantics that’s a little confusing but makes some sense if you know what to look out for.

StatementResponseStatementResponse

While it may test true that a given value is less than another (for example, one is less than four), another may test true to be only a little less (one is a little less than two). The same is true for other subjective comparisons like greater (9 is greater than 5, and 9 is a little greater than 8), and other magnitudes (1 is a little less than 1.1, and 9 is a little greater than 8.9).

What makes things even more confusing is that the delineation between less and a little less, for example, may change. A number may be less than another on one day, but a little less on another. Why they change I have no idea. The examples shown here were true for me at one point, but may become false later on.

The takeaway here is to remember that subjective magnitude may be important when determining sufficient truthfulness of comparison between numbers, but it’s important to not attach a value comparison to a particular subjective magnitude. For example, don’t become too attached to 1 being less than 900 but a lot less than 901, because tomorrow those values will likely be different.

Interestingly, the mathematical less than and greater than signs are not bound to the limitations of subjective magnitude. In other words, regardless of the difference in magnitude, the response makes sense. 1 < 2, 3, 4, 1 thousand, 1 billion, etc. They all test true.
