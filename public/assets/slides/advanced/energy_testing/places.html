Numbers - Determining the number of places in a number:

When the range of a number is unknown, I use the following method to determine it.

The short list below is very helpful when determining the number of places in a number:

one, ten, hundred, thousand, million, X-illion, X-illion^Y

Here's the general process I use:

1) Determine the lowest amount that's above the number being sought.

2) Determine the number of places necessary to be determined.

Here are some examples:

To determine the number 8, I would first determine the range of the number - 10, and then finish by using the digit determination method I described on the previous slide.

To determine the number 23, I would first determine the range - 100, and then I'd use the digit determination method for each of the two places.

To determine the number 52,340,000, I would first determine the range - million. I would then determine whether it's 1, 10, or 100 million, and find that it's 100 million. I would then determine the number of places needed to determine - 4. Then use the digit determination method on the previous slide for each of those places.

X - illion is short for billion, trillion, quadrillion, etc.
